You've Heard of a Food Desert. What About a Food Swamp?

By Amanda Braitman

October 6, 2022

A food swamp is a community that is oversaturated with unhealthy food options. It's a place where finding accessible, fresh and healthy food can often be a physical and economical struggle. East New York is one such place.

Residents of the Brooklyn neighborhood are suffering from elevated rates of nutrition-related illnesses such as obesity, hypertension and diabetes. According to the Community District Board, city efforts to bring more grocery stores, healthier bodega options, and better nutritional education to communities in need have been largely unsuccessful.

In 2015-2016, the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene conducted a Community Health Survey. Here is what they found:

Fast Food Proliferation

East New York resident Sharon Pitman, 58, has to take a cab to the nearest ShopRite to get anything fresh, despite living down the street from a bodega that should be stocking plenty of healthy food choices because it is part of the Shop Healthy NYC! Program that was started by the department of Health and Mental Hygiene in 2012.

When asked what the grocery shopping experience is like for her, Pitman replied in one emphatic word: "Expensive."

Shevanne Blake, 18, who lives in East New York and works at a local farmer's market, said he ate a lot of fast food growing up but realized all the problems it causes. "I saw a lot more junk food than actual vegetables... Every now and then you turn around and there's a new fast food area here," he said.

Blake said inflated prices are pushing people to buy cheap fast food, and that the produce available in supermarkets and bodegas is often moldy. "People don't know where to look" for fresh produce in his neighborhood, he said.
